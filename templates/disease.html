Understanding Heart Disease

Heart disease refers to any condition affecting the heart. According to the CDC, it is the leading cause of death globally. Early detection and healthy lifestyle choices are critical for prevention.

Common Types of Heart Disease

Coronary Artery Disease

The most common type. It develops when the arteries that supply blood to the heart become clogged with plaque, causing them to harden and narrow, reducing oxygen to the heart muscle.

Arrhythmia

An irregular heartbeat. It occurs when the electrical impulses do not work properly, causing the heart to beat too fast (Tachycardia) or too slowly (Bradycardia).

Myocardial Infarction

Also known as a heart attack, it involves a sudden interruption of the blood flow to the heart, damaging or destroying part of the heart muscle.

Congenital Heart Defects

Conditions people are born with, including hole in the septal wall or missing valves that require monitoring.

Symptoms to Watch For

A buildup of fatty plaques causes narrowed blood vessels that lead to angina or stroke. Men are likely to have chest pain, while women often experience shortness of breath and extreme fatigue along with chest discomfort.

  • Chest pain (angina)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pain in the neck or jaw
  • Fluttering in chest
  • Lightheadedness
  • Fainting (syncope)
  • Swelling of legs
  • Fatigue
  • Dry persistent cough

Prevention and Risk Factors

Primary Risk Factors

  • Age & Sex: Growing older increases risk. Men are at greater risk; women's risk spikes post-menopause.
  • Family History: Genetics heavily influence early onset cases.
  • Smoking: Nicotine tightens blood vessels globally.
  • Hypertension & Diabetes: Underlying metabolic diseases accelerate vascular wear.
  • Obesity & Inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle correlates highly.

Prevention Steps

Certain congenital conditions cannot be prevented, however, the majority of heart disease cases can be mitigated through lifestyle changes:

  • Quit smoking immediately.
  • Exercise at least 30 minutes daily.
  • Consume diets low in salt and saturated fats.
  • Manage stress effectively.
  • Practice good dental hygiene (poor teeth link to endocarditis).
